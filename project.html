Why this project matters

Aviation noise is one of the most widespread environmental problems associated with modern air travel. Millions of people living near airports are regularly exposed to noise levels that affect sleep, concentration, and long-term health. Engine noise is a major contributor — and reducing it is one of the central challenges in aircraft engineering.

The quietest part of a modern jet engine is not the fan or the turbine — it is a thin layer of carefully engineered material bonded to the inside of the engine casing. These are acoustic liners, and making them work better is what LINERFUN is about.

Acoustic liners work like tiny sound traps: they are arrays of small cavities, each tuned to absorb specific sound frequencies produced by the engine. They have been used in aircraft for decades and remain the most effective passive solution for reducing engine noise at source.

The challenge is that next-generation engines — known as Ultra-High Bypass Ratio (UHBR) engines — are designed to be shorter and wider, which means the inner surface available for liners shrinks considerably. At the same time, the noise targets are becoming stricter. Designers therefore need every square centimetre of liner to perform as well as possible.

To achieve this, engineers need accurate tools that can predict how a liner will behave under realistic conditions — in particular, when a strong airflow passes over its surface at the same time as sound waves are trying to penetrate it. This coupled problem is surprisingly difficult to solve, and the methods currently used in industry rely on simplifications that can miss important physical effects.

LINERFUN develops a new generation of simulation tools — specifically, boundary conditions for high-fidelity computational simulations — that capture this physics accurately and are practical enough to be used in engineering design workflows.

The research approach

The core technical contribution of LINERFUN is a set of physics-informed Time-Domain Impedance Boundary Conditions — a mouthful, but the idea is straightforward. When running a high-fidelity computer simulation of airflow inside an engine duct, you need a way to tell the simulation how the liner surface behaves acoustically. The boundary condition does exactly that: it translates the physical properties of the liner into a mathematical instruction that the simulation can use at every timestep.

The challenge is that existing boundary conditions were designed for simpler, frequency-domain methods. Adapting them to time-domain, scale-resolving simulations — which are far more accurate but computationally demanding — without losing physical consistency is a non-trivial problem. LINERFUN addresses it by embedding physical constraints directly into the model, and by using machine learning to extend its validity to conditions that are difficult to characterise analytically.

The approach is validated progressively against a hierarchy of test cases, starting from well-understood one-dimensional benchmarks and building up to realistic duct configurations with experimental data.

Objective 1

Robust boundary conditions

Develop causality-consistent, numerically stable liner boundary conditions compatible with large-eddy and scale-adaptive solvers.

Objective 2

Physics-informed machine learning

Use data-driven techniques to extend the models to non-linear operating conditions while preserving interpretability.

Objective 3

Liner design for UHBR engines

Apply the framework to realistic next-generation liner geometries and produce design guidelines transferable to industry.

Institutions involved
  • Beneficiary
    M1–3 & M25–36
    Turin, Italy

    Politecnico di Torino

    One of Italy's leading engineering universities, ranked 41st globally in Engineering and Technology (QS 2025). The host department, Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering (DIMEAS), runs the LINING project — an ERC-funded research programme on acoustic liners with which LINERFUN is closely connected. Supervisor: Prof. Francesco Avallone.

  • Secondment
    M4–7
    Toulouse, France

    ONERA — The French Aerospace Lab

    France's national aerospace research centre, founded in 1946. ONERA's Branch B2A in Toulouse specialises in the experimental characterisation of acoustic liners and their modelling. The team offers access to a dedicated wind tunnel for aeroacoustic testing, where LINERFUN will contribute to a joint experimental campaign. Supervisor: Dr. Estelle Piot.

  • Outgoing host
    M8–24
    Melbourne, Australia

    University of Melbourne

    Ranked 12th globally (QS), the Energy Institute's Computational Mechanics group leads the development of HiPSTAR — a high-performance simulation code for turbulent flows and aeroacoustics, which is the primary tool used in LINERFUN. The group also develops the machine learning framework EVE3 used for data-driven model enhancement. Supervisor: Prof. Richard Sandberg.
