Background & Motivation

Why Acoustic Liners?

Aviation noise is a major environmental concern near airports. Acoustic liners — arrays of resonant cavities bonded to the inner walls of turbofan nacelles — are the most effective passive technology for attenuating engine noise. As turbofan designs evolve towards Ultra-High Bypass Ratio (UHBR) architectures, shorter and wider nacelles shrink the available liner surface area, placing greater demand on liner efficiency per unit area.

Designing optimal liners requires accurate prediction of sound absorption in the presence of grazing flow — a coupled aeroacoustic problem. Current industrial tools rely on simplified frequency-domain methods that fail to capture non-linear and transient effects. High-fidelity scale-resolving CFD/CAA methods offer much higher accuracy but lack robust, physically consistent boundary conditions for liner surfaces.

The LINERFUN Approach

piTDIBC Methodology

LINERFUN develops physics-informed Time-Domain Impedance Boundary Conditions (piTDIBC): a new class of boundary conditions that embed acoustic liner physics directly into scale-resolving CFD/CAA solvers. The method combines:

Time-Domain Impedance Models

Causality-consistent impedance formulations (based on the Shur et al. framework) that support broadband liner responses across realistic operating conditions.

Scale-Resolving Simulations

Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Scale-Adaptive Simulation (SAS) on HPC platforms (CINECA LEONARDO, NCI Australia) for high-fidelity flow–acoustics coupling.

Physics-Informed Machine Learning

Data-driven techniques to extend impedance models and accelerate parameter optimisation, informed by physical constraints from acoustic theory.

Validation Hierarchy

Progressive validation from 1D analytical benchmarks (Casadei 2020) to NASA GIT duct experiments and the LINING test case (Paduano et al. 2024).

Objectives

Scientific Goals

LINERFUN is structured around three interconnected objectives:

Objective 1 — TDIBC Development

Robust piTDIBC formulation for scale-resolving solvers

Derive causality-compliant, numerically stable time-domain impedance models compatible with compressible LES/SAS solvers. Validate against benchmark test cases.

Objective 2 — Physics-Informed Learning

Data-driven extension and calibration of impedance models

Use physics-constrained machine learning to enrich impedance models with non-linear effects and extend their validity to UHBR operating conditions.

Objective 3 — Application

Liner design for UHBR nacelles

Apply the developed framework to realistic UHBR liner geometries, generating guidelines and design tools transferable to industrial partners.
